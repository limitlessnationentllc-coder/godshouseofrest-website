What Is Baptism and How Do I Prepare? A Simple Guide

By the God's House of Rest team Reviewed by Pastor R. Tyrone Robertson Updated June 18, 2026 5 min read

Baptism is a public declaration of faith in Jesus, pictured through water — going under and rising again to symbolize leaving an old life behind and stepping into a new one. It doesn't earn your salvation; it celebrates and announces it. Here's what it means and exactly how to prepare for yours.

Quick Answer
  • What it is: An outward sign of an inward decision to follow Jesus.
  • Is it required for salvation? Salvation comes through faith; baptism is the celebration of it.
  • How to prepare: Talk with a pastor, invite your people, bring a towel and a change of clothes.

What does baptism actually mean?

The word "baptize" comes from a Greek word meaning to immerse or dip. When someone is lowered into the water and raised back up, it's a living picture of the gospel: the old self is buried, and a new life begins. It's one of the first steps Jesus asked His followers to take — a way of saying publicly, "I belong to Him now."

"We were buried therefore with Him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead, we too might walk in newness of life."

— Romans 6:4 (ESV)

Baptism doesn't make you saved — it celebrates that you are.

Do I have to be baptized to be saved?

Most Christian traditions teach that salvation comes through faith in Jesus, and that baptism is the outward expression of that faith rather than its cause. In other words, baptism is an act of obedience and joy — something Jesus both commanded and modeled — not a box you check to earn God's love. You're loved first; baptism is your glad response.

Immersion or sprinkling — what's the difference?

Churches practice baptism in different ways. Immersion means being briefly lowered fully under the water and lifted back up, picturing burial and resurrection. Sprinkling or pouring uses a small amount of water. Both express the same faith. At God's House of Rest, we practice full immersion — sometimes in our services and sometimes in joyful "Rebirth Celebrations" at the beach.

How do I prepare for my baptism?

  • Talk with a pastor. Share your story and ask any questions. We'll walk you through everything — just reach out.
  • Understand the step. Baptism is meaningful precisely because you understand what it represents.
  • Invite your people. Family and friends make the day a celebration. Many guests come to faith simply by witnessing one.
  • Pack the practical stuff: modest clothing you don't mind getting wet, a change of clothes, and a towel.
  • Prepare a sentence or two about why you're taking this step — you may be invited to share briefly.

What happens on the day?

It's simpler — and more joyful — than most people expect. You'll usually share a short testimony or affirm your faith, a pastor will say a few words, and then you'll be gently lowered into the water and raised back up. It takes only a moment, but it's a moment a church family never forgets. Expect cheering.

Ready to take the step?

If you've put your faith in Jesus and want to make it public, we'd be honored to walk with you. New to all of this? Start with what to expect on a first visit, learn more about who we are, or contact us to begin the conversation about baptism.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is baptism?

A public declaration of faith in Jesus, symbolized by water. Going under and rising again pictures dying to an old life and being raised into a new one. It celebrates salvation rather than earning it.

Do I have to be baptized to be saved?

Most Christians teach salvation comes through faith in Jesus, with baptism as the outward expression of that faith — an act of obedience and celebration Jesus commanded and modeled.

What's the difference between immersion and sprinkling?

Immersion lowers you fully under the water and back up, picturing burial and resurrection; sprinkling uses a small amount of water. Practices vary; we practice full immersion.

How do I prepare for baptism?

Talk with a pastor, understand the step, invite loved ones, and bring modest clothing you don't mind getting wet, a change of clothes, and a towel.

What happens during a baptism?

You'll share a brief testimony or affirm your faith, a pastor will speak, and you'll be gently lowered into the water and lifted back up — a quick, joyful moment the whole church celebrates.
