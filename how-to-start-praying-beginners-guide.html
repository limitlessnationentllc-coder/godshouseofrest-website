How to Start Praying: A Simple 5-Step Guide for Beginners

By the God's House of Rest team Reviewed by Pastor R. Tyrone Robertson Updated June 18, 2026 9 min read

To start praying, simply talk to God in your own honest words — the way you'd talk to a trusted friend. You don't need special language, a perfect setting, or any experience. Tell Him how you're really doing, thank Him for something, and ask for what you need. If you'd like a little structure, the five steps below give you a clear, beginner-friendly way to pray today.

The Short Version
  • Prayer is a conversation, not a performance — plain words are perfect.
  • A simple pattern (we'll use ACTS) gives your prayer shape when you're unsure.
  • Pick a time and place so prayer becomes a habit, not an emergency.
  • When words run out, pray Scripture — a Psalm or the Lord's Prayer.
  • Consistency beats eloquence. Keep coming back, however briefly.

If you've ever wanted to pray but stopped because you weren't sure you were "doing it right," take a breath. You're in good company. Many people — including lifelong churchgoers — have felt clumsy and unsure the first time they tried to talk to God. Here's the freeing truth: there is no wrong way to begin. Prayer was never meant to be a skill you master. It's a relationship you grow.

"Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God."

— Philippians 4:6 (ESV)

What is prayer, really?

Prayer is simply talking with God. That's it. It's not a magic formula, a ritual you have to get exactly right, or a performance for an audience. It's an honest, two-way relationship with a Father who already knows you and still wants to hear from you.

That reframe changes everything. You don't pray to impress God. You pray to be with Him — to bring your real self, mess and all, and to learn, over time, to hear His voice in return. Our whole church is built on that kind of honest coming to God; it's the heart of what Matthew 11:28 means when Jesus invites the weary to come to Him.

Step 1: Just start talking to God

The hardest part of prayer is usually the first sentence. So make it easy. Say, out loud or silently, something as plain as: "God, I've never really done this before. I'm not sure what to say, but I want to talk to You."

That's a real prayer. Honesty is the doorway. You don't need "thee" and "thou," you don't need to sound like anyone else, and you certainly don't need to have your life together first. Come as you are.

Step 2: Use a simple pattern when you're unsure (ACTS)

When a blank page feels intimidating, a pattern helps. One that beginners have used for generations is ACTS. It's not a rule — it's training wheels. Move through these four movements and your prayer will feel natural in no time.

LetterMeansTry saying…
AAdoration — praise who God is"God, thank You that You're good and that You're near."
CConfession — be honest about your wrongs"I'm sorry for ___. Please forgive me and help me change."
TThanksgiving — name your gratitude"Thank You for ___ today."
SSupplication — ask for what you need"Please help me with ___, and watch over ___."

Walk through those four in a few minutes and you've prayed a full, balanced prayer. Over time you won't need the acronym — it will simply become how you talk to God.

Step 3: Pick a time and a place

Prayer grows into a relationship the same way every relationship does: through regular, ordinary time together. So choose a moment you can repeat — the first few minutes of your morning, your commute, a pause at lunch, or the last minutes before sleep. Pair it with a spot: a chair, a porch, a parked car. The point isn't a perfect environment; it's a reliable rhythm.

Reflect

Pick one: What's a moment that already repeats in your day — coffee, the drive home, bedtime — that you could turn into your prayer time this week?

Step 4: Pray Scripture when words run out

Some days you'll have plenty to say. Other days you'll feel empty — and that's exactly when Scripture becomes a gift. The Psalms are a prayer book for every emotion: grief, joy, fear, anger, gratitude. The Lord's Prayer, which Jesus taught His own followers when they asked how to pray, is a perfect template you can pray word for word.

"Pray then like this: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven…"

— Matthew 6:9–13 (ESV) · Read Psalm 23 on YouVersion

Read it slowly. Pause on a phrase that stands out. Make the words your own. Praying Scripture is like learning a language by speaking with a fluent friend — soon the words start coming on their own.

Step 5: Keep going, even imperfectly

You will forget some days. Your mind will wander mid-sentence. You'll wonder if anything is happening. Keep going anyway. The goal was never a flawless streak — it's a growing friendship. A short, distracted prayer offered honestly is worth more than a polished one you never pray.

Common questions and fears about prayer

"What if I don't feel anything?"

Feelings come and go; prayer isn't measured by them. Plenty of deep, faithful prayers are prayed on numb, ordinary days. Show up anyway — the relationship is real even when the emotions are quiet.

"What if I don't know if God is listening?"

Scripture promises He is. Answers may come as yes, no, or wait, and rarely on our schedule — but no honest prayer is wasted or unheard.

"Do I have to pray out loud or close my eyes?"

No. Out loud, silent, eyes open while you drive — all of it counts. Those are helpful habits, not requirements.

A Prayer to Pray Right Now

"God, here I am. Thank You for being near, even when I can't feel it. I'm sorry for the things I've gotten wrong, and I'm grateful for more than I usually stop to notice. Today I bring You ___. Teach me to talk with You, and to listen. I'm trusting You with this. Amen."

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I start praying for the first time?

Talk to God in your own honest words, as you would to a trusted friend. No special language or perfect setting needed. Tell Him how you're doing, thank Him for something, and ask for what you need — the ACTS pattern (Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication) can guide you.

Do I have to use special words to pray?

No. God cares about your heart, not your vocabulary. Everyday language is welcome — prayer is a real conversation, not a performance.

What should I pray about when I don't know what to say?

Pray Scripture. Read a Psalm or the Lord's Prayer slowly and make the words your own. Telling God "I don't know what to say" is itself an honest prayer.

How long should I pray, and where?

There's no required length — even one honest minute counts. Many beginners start with five minutes at a consistent time and quiet place so it becomes a habit.

Does God really hear my prayers?

Yes. Scripture promises God hears those who come to Him. Answers may be yes, no, or wait, and may not come on our timeline — but no honest prayer goes unheard.
